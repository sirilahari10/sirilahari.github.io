The Day the Models Went Blind

What the 2026 oil crisis taught me about the silent failure mode that nobody talks about.

I was doom-scrolling at like 11pm when I first saw it.

The Strait of Hormuz—this narrow strip of water I actually had to Google the location of—effectively closed. Oil prices didn't gradually rise. They detonated. Brent crude went from $81 a barrel to nearly $128 in weeks.

I started thinking about the models. Because somewhere out there, in banks, trading desks, energy companies, hundreds of carefully built forecasting models had just been handed a future they had never seen before. And most of them had absolutely no idea.

The assumption nobody tells you about

Every forecasting model makes one quiet assumption that almost nobody talks about out loud: The future will behave like the past.

The patterns, the volatility, the statistical relationships—they'll roughly follow the same rules. This has a name: stationarity. And it's the invisible foundation that almost every time series model is built on.

Then February 28th happened. And that assumption just stopped being true. That's called a structural break. And it might be the most dangerous thing that can happen to a model running in production.

Figure 1: The model (flat line) stays "confident" while reality (spike) leaves it behind.

What the numbers actually showed

I pulled 578 trading days of real Brent crude data and trained an ARIMA model on everything up to February 28th. Then let it forecast forward into the crisis.

The model's forecast flatlined around $70, neat confidence interval, very sure of itself. Meanwhile, reality went to $118. The model wasn't broken or buggy. It was doing exactly what it was designed to do. The problem was the world it learned from no longer existed.

The part that really got me

Confirming a break after the fact is easy. But how quickly would a live model catch it? That's what CUSUM monitoring is for. You track the running total of forecast errors. When a model starts going systematically wrong in one direction, the cumulative error climbs. When it crosses a threshold, you get an alert.

The first alert triggered on March 5th. Five trading days after the Strait closed.

Then I tried something more interesting: I fed the full time series to the PELT algorithm (change point detection) with zero information. No break date, no context. It found the break on February 27th. One day off. No prior knowledge. Just pattern recognition.

The lesson

The original model wasn't bad. It was just loyal to a world that no longer existed. Structural breaks happen to every model eventually. A competitor launches. A regulation passes. A supply chain snaps. Most of them don't make headlines; they just quietly make your predictions wrong.

The models that survive long-term aren't the ones with the "best" architecture. They're the ones built by people who assumed they'd eventually be wrong, and built systems to notice. I find that more interesting than any benchmark.
